Abstract

In this document, we summarize the theory of open quantum systems as it is implemented in the Quantarhei package. Before it grows into a self-contained text, the following books should be consulted to gat a full picture: Volkhard May and Oliver Kühn, Charge and Energy Transfer in Molecular Systems, Wiley-VCH, Berlin, 2000 (and later editions), Shaul Mukamel, Principles of Nonlinear Spectroscopy, Oxford University Press, Oxford, 1995 and Leonas Valkunas, Darius Abramavicius and Tom\’aš Mančal, Molecular Excitation Dynamics and Relaxation, Wiley-VCH, Weinheim, 2013.

None.1 Bath Correlation Functions and Spectral Densities

None.1.1 Bath Correlation Function

Bath correlation function is defined as a two point correlation function of the bath part ΔV of the system-bath interaction operator, i.e. as
(None.1) C(t) = (1)/(ℏ2)\rm\rmTrB{U†B(t)ΔVUB(t)ΔVw\rmeq}
where w\rmeq is the equilibrium bath density operator, UB(t) is the bath evolution operator and the trace is taken over the bath degrees of freedom. The bath correlation function is a complex quantity and as such it has a real part
(None.2) C′(t) = (1)/(2)[C(t) + C*(t)]
and an imaginary part
(None.3) C′′(t) =  − (i)/(2)[C(t) − C*(t)]
so that
(None.4) C(t) = C′(t) + iC′′(t)

None.1.2 Spectral Density

A very important quantity is the Fourier transform of the bath correlation function
(None.5) C̃(ω) = ∞⌠⌡0\rmdt C(t)eiωt = 2\rmRe∞⌠⌡0\rmdt C(t)eiωt
It is sometimes referred to as ↓spectral density, but we will reserve this name for a different quantity. We will follow Ref. [Mukamel1995]. The Fourier transform C̃(ω) can be split into even and odd parts defined as
(None.6) C̃′(ω) = ∞⌠⌡ − ∞\rmdt C′(t)eiωt,  C̃′′(ω) = i∞⌠⌡ − ∞\rmdt C′′(t)eiωt
so that
(None.7) C̃(ω) = C̃′(ω) + C̃′′(ω)
It can be shown (see [Mukamel1995]) that
(None.8) C̃( − ω) = e − (ℏω)/(kBT)C̃(ω)
and
(None.9) C̃(ω) = [1 + coth(ℏω ⁄ 2kBT)]C̃′′(ω)
Due to the relation between positive and negative frequency values of the Fourier transform of the bath correlation function, we can define it completely through the odd function C̃′′(ω) which is a Fourier transform of the imaginary part of the correlation function.
